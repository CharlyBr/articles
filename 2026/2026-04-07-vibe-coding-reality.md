# Vibe coding — what it actually means after 20 years of writing code

The term "vibe coding" is everywhere. Every week, someone posts about how they built an entire app by just talking to an AI. No code. No debugging. Just vibes.

I've been a developer for over 20 years. I've been coding daily with an AI agent for the past 6 months. And I think most people get vibe coding wrong.

## Where it comes from

The term was coined by Andrej Karpathy in early 2025. His idea was simple: instead of writing code character by character, you describe what you want, and an AI writes it. You "give in to the vibes" and let the AI handle the implementation.

It went viral. Suddenly everyone was a vibe coder. Non-technical founders claimed they built full SaaS products overnight. Twitter threads showed entire apps created in a single prompt. The hype machine went into overdrive.

But here's what got lost in the noise: Karpathy is a world-class engineer. When he vibes, he knows exactly what good code looks like. He can steer. He can catch mistakes. He has decades of intuition guiding every decision.

That context matters.

## My path to this moment

I wrote my first lines of code in the early 90s. BASIC, then Pascal, then C. I went through Epitech, spent years as a developer, then a sysadmin, then both at the same time. Linux, open source, infrastructure — I was always at the intersection of writing software and running it.

Over two decades, I worked across different stacks, different industries, different roles. Backend, frontend, DevOps, architecture. Each project taught me something about how software really gets built — not in theory, but in practice.

A few years ago, I started building my own products on the side. Small SaaS tools, side projects, experiments. The classic solo founder journey: too many ideas, not enough time. The bottleneck was always the same — implementation speed. I could design and architect faster than I could type.

Then AI coding tools showed up. First Copilot, then more advanced agents. And everything shifted.

## What vibe coding actually looks like

I use Claude Code every day. Not as a fancy autocomplete — as an actual agent that reads my codebase, writes code, runs tests, and proposes solutions.

Here's what a real session looks like:

I describe what I want to build. The agent scans the project, understands the framework, the patterns, the conventions. It proposes an approach. I review it, adjust the direction, and it implements. We iterate. Sometimes it gets it right on the first try. Sometimes I have to course-correct three or four times.

It's not magic. It's a conversation with a very fast, very knowledgeable junior developer who never gets tired.

What surprised me most is what changed and what didn't.

**What changed:** I prototype in hours instead of weeks. I explore ideas I would have dismissed as "too much work." I spend my time on architecture and decisions instead of syntax and boilerplate. The gap between having an idea and testing it in production has collapsed.

**What didn't change:** I still need to understand every line of code that ships. Bad requirements still produce bad software — just faster. The last 20% still takes 80% of the effort. And debugging is still debugging, whether you wrote the code or an AI did.

The real shift isn't about writing less code. It's about where the bottleneck sits.

It used to be implementation. Now it's decision quality.

## What this means

Vibe coding, for someone with experience, is a genuine productivity leap. Not because the AI replaces your skills — but because it amplifies them. Every architectural intuition, every pattern you've internalized over the years, becomes leverage. You spend less time on the mechanical part and more time on the part that actually matters: deciding what to build, how to structure it, and when to stop.

For someone without that experience, it's a trap. You can generate a lot of code very fast with no idea if it's correct, secure, or maintainable. The vibes feel great until you hit production.

The term "vibe coding" suggests casualness. In practice, it requires more discipline, not less. You need to review harder, think further ahead, and be ruthless about quality — because the speed makes it tempting to ship before you should.

## Where I see this going

We're at the very beginning. The tools improve every month. The workflows are still being invented. What I do today with Claude Code will look primitive in a year.

But the fundamental dynamic won't change: AI amplifies what you already know. It doesn't replace the need to know it.

If you're a developer wondering whether to dive in — do it. Not because it's the future (it is), but because it genuinely changes how you work. The learning curve is real, but so is the payoff.
